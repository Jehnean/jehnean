Driveline Baseball

Discovery

Drivelinebaseball.com is an e-commerce website dedicated to helping pitchers develop healthy, high velocity skills through their unique, metrics driven training programs. Driveline offers articles, equipment, and training curriculums athletes and coaches of many different ages, skill sets, and locations.

  • Team: Product manager, developer, and me
  • My role: Pitch to delivery, user research and interaction design
  • Timeline: 4 weeks from Oct 2015

The challenges

Just a blog update…

Initially, Driveline Baseball posted that they were just looking for help “updating their blog.” Digging in, it turned out to five very different sites linking together for one company.

Which program?

With training online or in person, for high school or college athletes, youth baseball and parents, and coaches, customers were confused about what options were available and best for them.

High bounce rates

The bounce rates on the sister sites were very high, 70% or more compared to less than 10% for the main site. Google Analytic metrics indicated the main source of traffic was the blog for “free how to” articles.

Research and planning

Gathering data about site visitors

Google Analytic metrics indicated that the main source of traffic for Driveline’s new and returning visitors were largely search related visits into the blog section of their website for “free how to” content followed by high exit rates. Using Lucky Orange over the course of a few weeks, we passively monitored thousands of users during their website sessions.

In addition to high exit rates on the homepage, we noted some of the high traffic webpages with significant exit rates. The major thing those pages had in common was that they were a different website with a different style and URL, despite being part of Driveline Baseball.

Using heat and click mapping tools, we confirmed that site visitors were self identifying as an athlete or coach. However, less than 20% of site visitors were reaching this section of the homepage.

This data helped reaffirm our hypothesis that the majority of site visitors were: not finding sufficient resources on the homepage, were self identifying as athletes or coaches, leaving the site immediately after being redirected to an unfamiliar website, and not provided relevant next steps from blog articles.

Customer personas

Our qualitative contextual inquiries in conjunction with keyword data and other analytics metrics helped guide the creation of user personas, and a design approach to reduce drop off with the goal of increasing conversions. This was key to organizing learning materials, courses, and products relevant audiences with unique priorities, mindsets, and needs.
